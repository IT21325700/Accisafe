The rapid growth of urbanization and vehicular density has brought forth critical concerns regarding road safety on a global scale. With expanding infrastructure, congested traffic conditions, and increasing reliance on vehicular transportation, road traffic accidents have emerged as one of the leading causes of death and injury worldwide. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), approximately 1.35 million people lose their lives every year due to road traffic crashes, and an estimated 20–50 million more sustain non-fatal injuries, often resulting in long-term disabilities. Road traffic injuries are the leading cause of death for children and young adults aged 5–29 years, which emphasizes the gravity of the situation.

Globally, the impact of road traffic accidents is not only measured by fatalities but also by the significant economic burden they place on countries. According to WHO, the economic cost of road traffic injuries is estimated to be between 1% and 3% of a country’s gross domestic product (GDP). Low- and middle-income countries are particularly affected, accounting for more than 90% of global road traffic deaths despite having approximately 60% of the world's vehicles. This disparity is largely due to the lack of robust road safety infrastructure, inadequate enforcement of traffic laws, and limited emergency response systems.

In Sri Lanka, road traffic accidents represent a major public health and safety issue. Statistics from the Sri Lanka Police Department and the National Council for Road Safety indicate that on average, six to eight fatalities occur every day due to road accidents. Annually, Sri Lanka reports over 3,000 deaths from traffic-related incidents, with tens of thousands more suffering injuries of varying severity. Despite legislative measures and awareness campaigns, the number of accidents remains high, raising concerns over the effectiveness of existing road safety mechanisms.

Research Gap

Following areas are the research gaps found in most of the recent researches.

Accident Detection

Traditional accident detection systems rely heavily on manual reporting, in-vehicle sensors, or witness intervention, which often leads to delayed emergency responses. These delays can worsen injury outcomes and contribute to secondary accidents. Although machine learning and computer vision technologies have shown potential in real-time detection using CCTV feeds, many urban areas still lack systems that can automatically identify accidents and notify responders with minimal latency. There is a clear gap in deploying AI-powered accident detection models on edge devices, which can perform on-site analysis and eliminate reliance on cloud processing delays.

Fire detection

Conventional fire detection in accident zones relies on static sensors or human reporting, which are insufficient in outdoor or large-scale accident environments. While drones and thermal cameras have been explored for forest or industrial fire detection, their application in urban accident scenarios remains underutilized. Most systems do not integrate AI-powered fire detection with drone surveillance to assess accident scenes in real time. Additionally, long-range, low-power communication protocols like LoRa, which are ideal for transmitting drone data in smart city environments, are rarely implemented alongside AI analytics—creating a critical gap in rapid, mobile fire hazard identification and response.

Traffic and Crime Management

Although some smart traffic systems use predictive analytics to manage congestion, they often operate independently of real-time incident data. There is limited research on integrating live accident and fire detection into adaptive traffic control systems that dynamically change signal patterns to ease congestion. Similarly, current crime detection and surveillance systems typically function in silos, without linking to accident data that could indicate hit-and-runs or other criminal behavior. Furthermore, there is a lack of secure, decentralized platforms that enable real-time, encrypted data sharing across traffic control, emergency services, and law enforcement. This disconnect creates inefficiencies in coordinated emergency response and public safety efforts.

Research Problem & Solution

Proposed Problem

How can develop a fully integrated Accident and Fire Detection System that leverages AI, drones, and IoT to improve safety, optimize emergency response, and improve urban traffic management in Smart Cities?

In urban areas, accident detection and emergency response are often delayed due to reliance on manual reporting and isolated systems. These delays increase the risk of fatalities, secondary accidents, and traffic congestion. Traditional fire detection and crime monitoring are also not integrated with accident response, limiting overall effectiveness. There is a critical need for a unified, real-time system that uses machine learning, drones, and IoT to detect accidents, fires, and related crimes instantly—ensuring faster emergency response, improved traffic flow, and enhanced urban safety.


Proposed Solution

overcome the limitations of traditional accident response systems, this research proposes an integrated Accident Identification and Notification System powered by Machine Learning (ML), drones, and IoT communication. The system uses deep learning models deployed on edge devices (like Raspberry Pi) to analyze live CCTV footage and detect accidents in real time. Once an incident is detected, it triggers automated alerts to emergency responders with minimal latency. Additionally, drones equipped with thermal and infrared cameras are deployed to monitor accident scenes for fire hazards. These drones use AI-powered image processing and LoRa communication for long-range, reliable data transfer. The system also includes predictive traffic control to dynamically adjust signal timings and reduce congestion around accident sites. A crime detection module with anomaly detection algorithms monitors for behaviors like hit-and-runs, while a blockchain-based data-sharing framework ensures secure communication between emergency services, law enforcement, and smart city infrastructure. This unified, intelligent platform aims to reduce emergency response times, improve traffic flow, and enhance public safety in urban environments.

Research Objectives

Accident Detection

The primary objective is to develop a real-time accident detection system that leverages machine learning and computer vision to analyze live CCTV footage. By deploying trained deep learning models on edge devices such as Raspberry Pi, the system will be capable of instantly identifying road accidents and triggering automated alerts to emergency responders. This will significantly reduce response time and improve the chances of saving lives and preventing secondary accidents.

Fire Detection

This objective focuses on implementing a drone-based fire monitoring module capable of identifying fire hazards at accident scenes. Drones equipped with thermal and infrared cameras will be integrated with AI-powered image processing algorithms to detect fires even under low-visibility conditions. Real-time alerts will be sent through LoRa communication to emergency services, enhancing situational awareness and improving the speed and accuracy of fire response operations.

Crime Detection

This objective involves enhancing urban safety through the integration of machine learning-driven behavior analysis for crime detection. The system will monitor surveillance footage and sensor inputs to detect anomalies such as hit-and-run incidents or suspicious behavior at accident scenes. By correlating accident data with real-time surveillance, it supports law enforcement with timely, actionable insights for incident investigation and response.

Smart Traffic System

The system aims to optimize urban traffic flow by dynamically adjusting signal timings based on real-time accident and congestion data. Predictive models trained on historical traffic patterns and live inputs will identify accident-prone zones and current traffic density. This component will help minimize traffic build-up, reduce travel delays, and ensure safer road conditions following an incident.

Methodology

Figure 3. High Level Architecture of the System.

The proposed system consists of 4 main components. They are;

  1. Stress Prediction Through Deep Learning.
  2. AI based self reflective journaling and activity suggestion.
  3. Facial Emotion Recognition & Video Recommendation.
  4. Generate custom images from user text input.

The proposed system integrates four core components—accident detection, fire detection, behavior analysis, and predictive traffic management—into a unified, intelligent emergency response platform. The methodology begins with real-time data collection from CCTV cameras and drone surveillance. CCTV footage is analyzed through machine learning models, primarily Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), to detect accidents and identify risky driving behaviors. The footage undergoes preprocessing steps such as noise reduction and frame normalization to improve model accuracy and ensure timely detection.

For fire detection, drones equipped with thermal and infrared cameras are deployed to scan accident zones. These drones capture real-time imagery, which is then processed by trained ML models to detect fire hazards. The collected data is transmitted using low-power, wide-area network protocols such as LoRa for reliable, long-distance communication.Simultaneously, behavioral analysis is performed using real-time surveillance and anomaly detection models to identify suspicious activities or accident-related crimes, such as hit-and-run cases. This module strengthens situational awareness and supports law enforcement through the detection of unusual behavioral patterns that may indicate criminal activity.All collected and processed data is centralized through a control system that serves as an interface between traffic management systems, emergency services, and government authorities. Predictive analytics modules within the system analyze historical and real-time traffic data to dynamically adjust signal timings and prevent congestion near the incident area. Data is stored in a secure database and shared across components via a decentralized architecture, ensuring confidentiality and real-time communication. The entire system is tested in simulated high-traffic environments, evaluating parameters such as detection accuracy, response speed, and coordination efficiency. Iterative feedback is used to fine-tune the models and optimize system performance for real-world deployment in smart city infrastructures.
